Notes & Answers to Questions

  • Av is irregular because it's masculine but when we add suffixes to it to make other words then we use feminine suffixes even though the word itself is masculine. For example, the plural form is avot, not avim.
  • Abba is both the informal word for a father (which we usually translate as "dad" or "daddy") and the Aramaic equivalent of av. In the prayerbook you will see av rather than abba.
  • Most words in Hebrew are labelled as either masculine or feminine. First person words like ani are an exception.
  • The literal translation of Moshe navi is "Moses prophet". The joining words, "is a", or "are" are implicit in Hebrew.
  • The abbreviation יְיָ is short for the letters Y-H-V-H, which is the most holy name of God. I've written it in full in the next section. Both forms are irregular because the consonants written don't reflect the pronunciation. When we read them we say Adonai.
  • Most words are either masculine or feminine but adjectives have both a masculine version and a feminine version. For example, we have both tov and tovah.
  • L'Shanah Tovah can be shortened to, "Shanah Tovah!", i.e. "Good Year!"
  • G'mar-chatimah tovah is a greeting for Yom Kippur.
